    Phylogenetic Tree

    Node
    Represents averaged ancesterol state, not single organisms.
    Internal Node
    Contains sub-nodes.
    Represents a population common to two different branches.
    Terminal
    Outermost point.
    Represents specific organisms.
    Branch
    Organisms of the same branch share the same feature or trait.
    Ancestral Branch
    Continuation of the last trait.
    Derived Branch
    New traits branch off.
    Horizontal Gene Transfer
    Organisms engulfing others and creating organelles -> genes swap between branches.
    LUCA
    Last Universal Common Ancestor
    Rooted Tree
    All organisms have a common ancestor, known as the out-group.
    Un-Rooted Tree
    No known common ancestor.
    Relative measure of how closely related they are.
    Protists
    Single-celled "microbial eukaryotes".
    Clade
    Ancestral population and all its descendant populations.
    Every child node from an internal node (including all terminal species under those nodes).
    Sister Clade
    Most closely related clades.
    Sister Species
    Most closely related species.
    Cladograms
    Doesn't have a time reference.
    Apomorphy Derived Trait
    A novel character or character state that has evolved from its ancestral form.
    Synapomorphy
    Trait present in a common ancestor and shared exclusively by its descendants.
    • Trait evidence of shared ancestry.
    Sometimes a synapomorphy is lost during evolution

    Snakes lost their 4 legs from ancestors.

    Study Guide

    1. How is a phylogenetic tree useful a. What is a synapomorphy? b. What is a node? What happens at a node?
    2. What is the difference between a phylogenetic tree and a cladogram?
    3. In a phylogenetic tree (but not a cladogram) what does the length of a branch indicate?
    4. How does a node relate to a synapomorphy? a. Branches: what does ancestral mean, and what does derived mean.
    5. What is LUCA? Is it a specific species?
    6. What are the three main domains of life?
    7. Why are there so many different types of “Trees of Life?”
    8. Of the Prokaryotes: are Archaea or Bacteria more closely related to Eukaryotes?
    9. What is an ‘extant’ species what is an ‘extinct’ species?
    10. What is a “rooted” phylogenetic tree? a. What is the root? b. What is an ‘outgroup’?
    11. What species do you use as the ‘outgroup’ in an unrooted tree?
    12. What can happen at nodes, that makes phylogenetic trees or cladograms confusing?
    13. What is a clade? On any of the trees shown in the slides, circle a clade.
    14. On a cladogram, be able to identify types of clades: a. Paraphyletic group b. Monophyletic group c. Polyphyletic group
    15. Which of the three types of groups above is useful to classify organisms?
    16. If two different types of animals have similar traits, what are the two ways that may have occurred?
    17. What are two types of homologous features, give an example of each (think of synapomorphies!).
    18. What drives the development of Analogous features in different animals?
    19. Scenario: two animals are in the same monophyletic clade, but one does not have a synapomorphy that is hallmark of the clade – how could that happen?
    20. How can snakes and lizards be classified closely together when snakes don’t have legs? a. Compare this to marine mammals: why is a whale classified with humans, bats, horses, etc.?
    21. Name the types of data we use to classify organisms?
    22. What is the Rule of Parsimony?
