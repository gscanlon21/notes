    Anatomic Reference Systems

    Anatomy is defined as the study of structures of the body. The term anatomical, thus refers to anatomy. Therefore, studying bones, muscles or the brain is the study of anatomy.

    Anatomical position is a person standing erect, facing forward with their hands at the side, with face, palms and toes facing forward as depicted.

    Anatomy
    The branch of morphology concerned with the study of the internal and external structure of organisms and their parts.
    Anatomical Position
    Standard reference position used for describing locations and directions on the human body.

    Body Planes

    Frontal (Coronal)
    Divides the body or an organ into an anterior (front) portion and a posterior (rear) portion.
    The frontal plane is often referred to as a coronal plane. (“Corona” is Latin for “crown.”)
    Sagittal
    Also known as the midline.
    Divides the body, from the top to bottom, into equal left and right halves.
    Divides the body or an organ vertically into right and left sides.
    If this vertical plane runs directly down the middle of the body, it is called the midsagittal or median plane.
    If it divides the body into unequal right and left sides, it is called a parasagittal plane or less commonly a longitudinal section.
    Transverse
    The horizontal plane that divides the whole body into upper and lower portions.
    Divides the body or organ horizontally into upper and lower portions.
    Transverse planes produce images referred to as cross sections.

    Body Directions

    Anterior, Ventral
    Refers to the front or belly-side of the body or organ.
    Describes the front or direction toward the front of the body.
    Posterior, Dorsal
    Refers to the back of the body or organ.
    Describes the back or direction toward the back of the body.
    Superior, Cranial
    Describes a position above or higher than another part of the body proper.
    Inferior, Caudal
    Toward the lower part of the body.
    Describes a position below or lower than another part of the body proper.
    Proximal
    Situated nearest the midline or beginning of a body structure.
    Describes a position in a limb that is nearer to the point of attachment or the trunk of the body.
    Distal
    Situated farthest from the midline or beginning of a body structure.
    Describes a position in a limb that is farther from the point of attachment or the trunk of the body.
    Lateral
    Describes the side or direction toward the side of the body.
    Superficial
    Describes a position closer to the surface of the body.
    Deep
    Describes a position farther from the surface of the body.
    Cephalic seh-FAL-ick
    Toward the head.

    Body Cavities

    The two major body cavities are the dorsal and ventral cavities.

    Dorsal
    Within the dorsal cavity we find the cranial cavity (contains brain) and the spinal cavity (contains the spinal cord).
    Ventral
    The ventral cavity contains the thoracic (contains lungs and heart), abdominal and pelvic cavities (sometimes referred to as the abdominopelvic cavity as there is no physical separation).

    Structures of the Body

    Cells

    Cells
    Are the smallest functional units of the human body.
    They are highly specialized as they perform various different functions.
    For example, a erythrocyte (red blood cell) is flexible and packed with oxygen as it’s function is to travel through the body exchanging gas while a neuron is branching because of its role in communication.
    Stem cells however are a special type of cell because they are unspecialized and can renew themselves. Stem cells are not yet differentiated into having a specialized function, but certain conditions can transform them to be differentiated cells with a specific function. Embryonic stems cells can form any adult cell.
    Cytology
    The study of cells.
    cyt meaning cell, -ology meaning study).

    Tissues

    A tissue is a group of cells working together to perform a similar function. Histology is the study of tissues (hist means tissue and -ology means the study of). There are four main types of tissue:

    Connective
    support and connects internal organs and other body tissues
    Epithelial
    forms protective coverings internally and externally and be found lining the skin, blood vessels, and lungs. Specialized groups of epitheial cells that are capable of producing secretions are termed glands. Two types exist: endocrine and exocrine. Endocrine produce hormones secreted into the bloodstream (ex. estrogen) vs exocrine glands which secrete into other organs or outside the body (ex. sweat).
    Muscle
    responsible for movement via cells that contract and relax and can be found in the heart, digestive tract and skeletal muscle connected to the bones
    Nerve
    responsible for communication in the body via cells that are responsive to stimuli and can conduct electrical signals

    Organ Systems

    Lymphatic System Thymus, Lymph nodes, Spleen, Lymphatic vessels
    Returns fluid to blood
    Defends against pathogens
    Respiratory System Nasal passage, Trachea, Lungs
    Removes carbon dioxide from the body
    Delivers oxygen to the blood
    Integumentary System Hair, Skin, Nails
    Encloses internal body structures
    Site of many sensory receptors
    Skeletal System Cartilage, Bones, Joints
    Supports the body
    Enables movement (with muscular system)
    Digestive System Stomach, Liver, Gall bladder, Large intestine, Small intestine
    Processes food for use by the body
    Removes wastes from undigested food
    Urinary System Kidneys, Urinary bladder
    Controls water balance in the body
    Removes wastes from blood and excretes them
    Muscular System Skeletal muscles, Tendons
    Enables movement (with skeletal system)
    Helps maintain body temperature
    Nervous System Brain, Spinal cord, Peripheral nerves
    Detects and processes sensory information
    Activates bodily responses
    Male Reproductive System Epididymis, Testes
    Produces sex hormones and gametes
    Delivers gametes to female
    Female Reproductive System Mammary glands, Ovaries
    Produces sex hormones and gametes
    Supports embryo/fetus until birth
    Produces milk for infant
    Endocrine System Pituitary gland, Thyroid gland, Pancreas, Adrenal gland, Testes, Ovaries
    Secretes hormones
    Regulates body processes
    Cardiovascular System Heart, Blood Vessels
    Delivers oxygen and nutrients to tissues
    Equalizes temperature in the body

    Pathology

    Pathology
    is the study of disease. It can also mean a condition produced by a disease. A pathogen is a microorganism which produces a disease such as the influenza (flu) virus.
    Etiology
    is the study of the causes of disease or abnormal condition.

    Disease can be transmitted (spread) in many ways such as direct (human to human contact), bloodborne (via infected blood or bodily fluid), airborne (via air), vector-borne (via insects).

    Diseases can also be classified in many ways such as infectious (caused by a living pathogenic organism), iatrogenic (result of medical treatment), idiopathic (no known cause), nosocomial (hospital acquired) diseases.

    Genetics

    Genetics is the study of how genes (the basic physical and functional unit of heredity; Genes are made up of DNA) are inherited from parents to children and the role of genes in health and disease (gene means producing, and -tics means pertaining to).

    Genetic Disorder, Hereditary Disease
    Is caused by an absent or defective gene.

    down syndrome, hemophilia, muscular dystrophy, and cystic fibrosis.
