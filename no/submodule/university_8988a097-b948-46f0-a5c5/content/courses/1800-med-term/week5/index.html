    Respiratory System

    Nose nas/o rhino/o
    Exchanges air during inhaling and exhaling.
    Warms, moisturizes, and filters inhaled air.
    Sinuses sinus/o
    Provides mucus.
    Makes bones of the skull lighter.
    Aids in sound production.
    Pharynx pharyng/o
    Transports air to and from the nose to the trachea.
    Larynx laryng/o
    Makes speech possible.
    Epiglottis epiglott/o
    Closes off the trachea during swallowing.
    Trachea trache/o
    Transports air to and from the pharynx to the bronchi.
    Bronchi bronch/o, bronchi/o
    Transports air from the trachea into the lungs.
    Alveoli alveol/o
    Air sacs that exchange gases with the pulmonary capillary blood.

    Medical Specialties of the Respiratory System

    Pulmonologist
    Physician who specializes in diagnosing and treating diseases and disorders of the respiratory system
    Respiratory Therapist
    Licensed medical professional who specializes in treating patients with breathing difficulties
    Otolaryngologist, ENT
    Ear, nose, and throat doctor.
    Physician who treats diseases and disorders of the head and neck.
    Thoracic Surgeon
    Physician who performs surgery on the organs of the thoracic cavity including the heart, lungs and esophagus

    Pathology of the Respiratory System

    Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease
    A group of diseases that cause airflow blockage and breathing-related problems and includes chronic bronchitis and emphysema.
    Upper Respiratory Infection
    Affects the upper part of your respiratory system, including your sinuses and throat and include the common cold, laryngitis, and pharyngitis (inflammation of the larynx and pharynx respectively).
    Allergic Rhinitis
    commonly known as an allergy, this is the inflammation of the nasal passages.
    Respiratory Syncytial Virus
    Highly contagious viral infection similar to the common cold but may be more severe in children and infants (bronchiolitis).
    Sinusitis
    Inflammation of the sinuses.
    Hemothorax
    Blood collection in the pleural cavity (cavity which contains lungs).
    Pneumothorax
    Air accumulation in the pleural space leading to a pressure imbalance that can collapse the lungs.
    Atelectasis
    Collapsed lung.
    Pulmonary Embolism
    A blockage, often a blood clot, that stops blood flow to an artery in the lung.
    Pulmonary Edema
    Fluid accumulation in the lungs.
    Tuberculosis
    Infectious bacterial disease that attacks the lungs.
    Asthma
    Chronic lung disease caused by inflammation and muscle tightening around the airways, which makes it harder to breathe.
    Influenza
    Also known as the flu, is a viral infection which manifests as respiratory inflammation, fever, chills, and muscle pain.
    Cystic Fibrosis
    Genetic disorder that produces excess mucus in the lungs.
    Sleep Apnea
    Potentially serious sleep disorder in which breathing repeatedly stops and starts.

    Digestive System

    Ingestion
    Food and water are brought into digestive system.
    Secretion
    Endocrine/exocrine glands secrete mucus, enzymes, acids, and hormones to aid digestive processes.
    Propulsion
    Pass from one digestive organ to next; peristalsis (rhythmic contracts of smooth muscle).
    Absorption
    Movement through wall of alimentary canal into blood or lymphatic vessels.
    Digestion
    Breakdown; mechanical (chewing) and chemical (via digestive enzymes).
    Defecation
    Non-digestible or unusable materials exit body as feces.

    Digestive System Structures

    Mouth or/o, stomat/o
    Begins preparation for food digestion (mechanical and chemical).
    Teeth odont/i
    Used for mastication (chewing) for mechanical digestion.
    Pharynx pharyng/o
    Transports food from mouth to esophagus.
    Esophagus esophag/o
    Transports food from pharynx to stomach.
    Stomach gastr/o
    Aids in chemical and mechanical digestion via secretions and propels it towards the small intestine.
    Small Intestine enter/o
    Mixes incoming food (chyme) with digestive juices (chemical digestion); location of the majority of nutrient absorption
    Large Intestine col/o, colon/o
    Absorbs excess water and prepares solid waste for elimination
    Rectum and Anus an/o, proct/o, rect/o
    Controls defecation.
    Liver hepat/o
    Secrets bile and enzymes for lipid digestion
    Gallbladder cholecyst/o
    Stores bile
    Pancreas pancreat/o
    Secretes digestive juices and enzymes into the small intestines

    Digestive Tract

    Oral Cavity (mouth)
    The oral cavity is responsible for ingestion, secretion, chemical and mechanical digestion, and propulsion.
    The oral cavity mechanically forms food into a mass called a bolus and delivers it to the pharynx.
    It contains two accessory organs, the teeth and tongue.
    Pharynx
    Responsible for propulsion of the bolus from the oral cavity towards the esophagus. The nasopharynx is solely a part of the respiratory system, while the lower pharynx which includes the oropharynx and laryngopharynx are a part of both the respiratory and digestive systems.
    Esophagus
    Responsible for deglutition (swallowing) and contains two sphincters which control the movement of the bolus and prevents regurgitation from the stomach.
    Stomach
    Sac-like organ with an additional muscular layer for mechanical digestion and rugae which are folds that allows for expansion.
    The stomach is responsible for propulsion, secretion of gastric juices, chemical and mechanical digestion.
    Small Intestine
    Location of the majority of nutrient absorption and consists of three regions, the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. Propels food towards the large intestine.
    Large Intestine
    Connects the small intestines to the anus and is responsible for water absorption and prepares solid waste for defecation.
    Anus
    Final portion of the digestive tract.
    Responsible for defecation.

    Accessory Organs

    Teeth-Dentition
    Key structures arranged in upper and lower jaw for mastication of food.
    Aids in chemical digestion by increasing the surface area of food.
    Tongue
    Strong skeletal muscle which contains taste buds and assists in mastication and propulsion.
    Liver
    Responsible for bile production which aids in fat digestion; also removes toxins from blood and breaks down hemoglobin from erythrocytes
    Gallbladder
    Site of bile storage.
    Pancreas
    Produces and secretes digestive juices which assist in neutralizing incoming acidic chyme to the small intestines

    Medical Specialties of the Digestive System

    Dentist
    Doctor of dental surgery (DDS)
    Doctor of medical dentistry (DMD)
    Treats diseases and disorders to the teeth and tissues of the oral cavity.
    Gastroenterologist
    Physician who specializes in diseases and disorders of the stomach and intestines.
    Orthodontist
    Dental specialist who prevents or corrects malocclusion of the teeth and related facial structures.
    Periodontist
    Dental specialist who prevents or treats disorders of the tissues surrounding the teeth.
    Proctologist
    Physician who specializes in disorders of the colon, rectum, and anus.

    Pathology of the Digestive System

    Canker Sores (aphthous ulcers)
    Gray-white pits with a red border in the soft tissues lining the mouth; associated with stress, certain foods, or fever
    Cleft Lip
    Birth defect; deep groove of the lip running upward to the nose
    Dental Caries
    Also known as tooth decay or a cavity.
    Caused by bacteria that destroy the enamel and dentin of the tooth
    Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
    Upward flow of acid from the stomach into the esophagus.
    Causes heartburn or pyrosis.
    Gastroenteritis
    Inflammation of the mucous membrane lining the stomach and intestines
    Emesis
    Also known as vomiting.
    Reflex ejection of the stomach contents outward through the mouth.
    Diverticulosis
    Chronic presence of an abnormal number of diverticula, or sacs, formed in weak spots of the colon
    Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
    Intermittent cramping and abdominal pain, accompanied by constipation or diarrhea; unknown cause all.
    Crohn’s Disease (CD)
    Autoimmune disorder; can occur anywhere in the digestive tract. Generally penetrates every layer of tissue in the affected area. Most common complication: blockage of the intestine
    Fecal Incontinence
    Inability to control the excretion of feces.
    Hemorrhoids
    Cluster of veins, muscles, and tissues slip near/through the anal opening
    Hepatitis
    Liver inflammation: usually a viral infection.
    Cholelithiasis
    Presence of gallstones in the gallbladder or bile ducts.
    Anal Fissure
    Small crack-like sore in the skin of the anus.
    Constipation
    Having a bowel movement fewer than three times per week.
    Diarrhea
    Abnormally frequent flow of loose or watery stools.
    Oral Thrush
    Fungal infection in the mouth caused by Candida albicans.
    Lactose Intolerance
    Inability to digest dairy.
    Absence of the enzyme lactase.
    Jaundice
    Yellow discoloration.
    High levels of bilirubin in the blood.
    Celiac Disease
    Autoimmune disorder.
    Severe reaction to ingesting gluten.

    Terms

    aerophagia ayr-oh-FAY-jee-ah
    The excess swallowing of air while eating or drinking, and is a common cause of gas in the stomach.
    anastomosis ah-nas-toh-MOH-sis
    A surgical connection between two hollow or tubular structures.
    anoxia ah-NOCK-see-ah
    The absence of oxygen from the body's gasses, blood, or tissues.
    antiemetic an-tih-ee-MET-ick
    A medication administered to prevent or relieve nausea and vomiting.
    antitussive an-tih-TUSS-iv
    A medication administered to prevent or relieve coughing.
    aphonia ah-FOH-nee-ah
    The loss of the ability of the larynx to produce normal speech sounds.
    ascites ah-SIGH-teez
    An abnormal accumulation of serous fluid in the peritoneal cavity.
    asphyxia ass-FICK-see-ah
    The condition that occurs when the body cannot get the air it needs to function.
    asthma AZ-mah
    A chronic allergic disorder, characterized by severe breathing difficulty, coughing, and wheezing.
    atelectasis at-ee-LEK-tah-sis
    A condition in which the lung fails to expand completely, because of shallow breathing, or because the air passages are blocked.
    bariatrics bayr-ee-AT-ricks
    The branch of medicine concerned with the prevention and control of obesity and allied diseases.
    bradypnea brad-ihp-NEE-ah
    An abnormally slow rate of respiration, usually of less than 10 breaths per minute.
    bronchiectasis brong-kee-ECK-tah-sis
    When the walls of your bronchi, the tubes that carry air into and out of your lungs, become thickened and damaged.
    bronchodilator brong-koh-dye-LAY-tor
    A medication that expands the opening of the passages into the lungs.
    bronchoscopy brong-KOS-koh-
    The visual examination of the bronchi with the use of a bronchoscope.
    bronchospasm BRONG-koh-spazm
    A contraction of the smooth muscle in the walls of the bronchi and bronchioles that tighten and squeeze the airway shut.
    cachexia kah-KEKS-eeh-ah
    A condition of physical wasting away due the loss of weight and muscle mass that occurs in patients with diseases such as advanced cancer or aids.
    canker sores KANE-ker
    Also known as a mouth ulcer or aphthous ulcer.
    A canker sore is a small, shallow, open wound or ulcer in your mouth that can make eating and talking uncomfortable.
    Celiac Disease SEE-lee-ak
    An autoimmune disorder triggered by gluten that damages the small intestine and affects nutrient absorption.
    cheilitis kye-LITE-is
    Lip Inflammation
    A common skin condition affecting the corners of your mouth. It leads to painful, cracked sores.
    cholangitis koh-lan-JIGH-tis
    An acute infection of the bile duct, characterized by pain in the upper right quadrant of the abdomen, fever, and jaundice.
    cholecystectomy koh-luh-sis-TECK-toh-mee
    The surgical removal of the gall bladder.
    cholecystitis koh-luh-sis-TYE-tis
    Inflammation of the gall bladder that is usually associated with gall stones blocking the flow of bile.
    cholelithiasis koh-luh-lih-THIGH-ah-sis
    The presence of gall stones in the gall bladder or bile ducts.
    chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
    A long-term lung condition that makes it progressively harder to breathe.
