    Functions of Special Senses

    Eyes opt/i, opt/o, optic/o, opthalm/o
    Receptor organs for the sense of sight.
    Iris ir/I, ir/o, irid/o, irit/o
    Controls the amount of light entering the eye.
    Lens phac/o, phak/o
    Focuses rays of light on the retina.
    Retina retin/o
    Converts light rays into electrical impulses to transmit to the brain.
    Lacrimal Apparatus dacryocyst/o, lacrim/o
    Accessory structure of the eyes that produce, store, and remove tears.
    Ears acous/o, acoust/o, audi/o, audit/o, ot/o
    Contains receptors for the sense of hearing as well as balance/equilibrium.
    Outer Ear pinn/i
    Transmits sound waves to the middle ear.
    Middle Ear myring/o, tympan/o
    Transmits sound waves to the inner ear.
    Inner Ear labyrinth/o
    Receives sound vibrations and transmits them to the brain; also contains receptors for equilibrium and balance.

    Eye Structures

    Fibrous Layer
    This outermost layer is protective for the eye and serves as the attachment site for muscles oblique and rectus muscles) which move the eye. This fibrous layer includes the
    Sclera Fibrous Layer
    The whites of the eye which is irregularly arrange collagen providing protection
    Cornea Fibrous Layer
    Continuous with the sclera but clear to allow light to pass through.
    It has no blood supply (avascular).
    Iris Vascular Layer
    Pigmented layer which surrounds the pupil and contains muscle which can control light passing through.
    Ciliary Body Vascular Layer
    ring of smooth muscle that surrounds lens and can control its shape for focusing of light
    Choroid Vascular Layer
    inner pigmented layer which has a rich blood supply
    Retina Neural Layer
    Contains photoreceptors (rods and cones) which are the receptors for light.
    The location with the highest number of photoreceptors and thus sharpest point of vision is the macula lutea with the fovea centralis.
    Optic nerve Neural Layer
    exits the neural layer at the optic disk (blind spot) and carries information about vision to the brain
    Eyelids, Eyebrows and Eyelashes
    Help protect the eyeball from foreign material, excessive light, and injuries.
    Conjunctiva
    Transparent mucous membrane; protects exposed eyeball surface.
    Lacrimal apparatus
    Contains glands which secrete tears into a canal to drain into the nasolacrimal duct.

    Functions of the Eye

    Accommodation
    Process by which eye makes adjustments for seeing objects near and far such as changing the shape of the lens.
    Convergence
    Simultaneous inward movement of the eyes toward each other to maintain singular binocular vision as an objects comes nearer.
    Visual Acuity
    The ability to distinguish object details and shape at a distance.
    Refraction
    The ability of the lens to bend light rays so they focus back on photoreceptors in the retina

    Medical Specialties of the Eye

    Ophthalmologist
    Physician who specializes in diagnosing and treating the full spectrum of diseases and disorders of the eyes, from vision correction to eye surgery.
    Optometrist
    Holds a doctor of optometry degree and provides primary eye car, including diagnosing eye diseases and conditions and measuring the accuracy of vision to determine whether corrective lenses are needed.
    Optician
    A health care practitioner who designs, fits and dispenses lens for vision correction.

    Pathology of the Eye

    Conjunctivitis
    Pinkeye
    Xerophthalmia
    Also known as dry eyes.
    Drying of eye surfaces, including the conjunctiva.

    Uvea, cornea, and sclera

    Cataract
    Loss of transparency of the lens.
    Diabetic Retinopathy
    Damage to the retina.
    Complication of uncontrolled diabetes.
    Photopsia
    Flashes of light.
    Retinal Detachment
    Also known as detached retina or retinal tears.
    Separation of some or all of the light-sensitive retina from the choroid.
    Retinoblastoma
    Cancer caused by the abnormal growth of retinal cells.
    Glaucoma
    A group of diseases causing increased intraocular pressure.
    Causes damage to the retinal nerve fibers and the optic nerve.
    Macular Degeneration (MD)
    A gradually progressive condition.
    Macula at the center of the retina is damaged.
    Diplopia
    Also known as double vision.
    Perception of two images of a single object.
    Color Blindness
    Inability to perceive certain colors.
    Photophobia
    Excessive sensitivity to light.
    Presbyopia
    Common changes in the eyes that occur with aging.
    Strabismus
    Also known as crossed eyes.
    Misalignment of the eyes, causing one eye to deviate inward (esotropia) toward the nose, or outward (exotropia), while the other eye remains focused.
    Astigmatism
    Eye does not focus properly.
    Uneven curvatures of the cornea.
    Hyperopia
    Also known as farsightedness.
    Light rays focus beyond the retina.
    Myopia
    Also known as nearsightedness.
    Light rays focus in front of the retina.

    Ear

    The Middle Ear
    Contains the three smallest bones in the body, termed the auditory ossicles.
    These are named the malleus, incus, and stapes. As sound waves arrive to the middle ear, it vibrates the tympanic membrane (eardrum) which then moves the ossicles, sort of like wind chimes. This is how sound is conducted from outer ear to inner ear. In addition, we find the eustachian tube (pharyngotympanic) which helps equalize pressure from the outside world to the inner ear.
    The Inner Ear
    Contains the snail shaped cochlea. The sound waves travel from the middle ear to the inner ear through the oval window and into the cochlea. This distortion of the cochlea triggers the cochlear nerve transmitting the sensation of sound to the brain. The inner ear also contains fluid-filled areas which sense changes in equilibrium and balance. These areas are termed the semicircular canals and vestibule.

    Medical Specialties of the Ear

    Audiologist
    Specializes in the measurement of hearing function and in the rehabilitation of persons with hearing impairments.
    Otolaryngologist
    Ear, nose and throat physician.
    Speech-Language Pathologist
    Can be involved in numerous medical areas including audiology; these practitioners help patients who have problems related to swallowing, speech and communication disorders.

    Pathology of the Ear

    Otalgia (earache)
    Pain in the ear.
    Otomycosis
    Fungal infection.
    Otopyorrhea
    Flow of pus from the ear.
    Otorrhea
    Any discharge from the ear.
    Otitis Media
    Inflammation of the middle ear.
    Otosclerosis
    Ankylosis of the bones of the middle ear.
    Vertigo
    Sense of whirling, dizziness, and loss of balance.
    Ménière’s Disease
    Relatively rare chronic disorder wherein the amount of fluid in the inner ear increases intermittently.
    Tinnitus
    Ringing, buzzing, or roaring sound in one or both ears.
    Acoustic Neuroma
    Benign brain tumor adjacent to the cranial nerve.
    Deafness
    Complete or partial loss of hearing.
    Conductive Hearing Loss
    Sound waves prevented from passing to the inner ear.
    Sensorineural Hearing Loss (nerve deafness)
    Damaged nerve or hair cells in the ear.

    Integumentary System

    There are six functions of the integument. I like to use the acronym “SHAPES” to help remember these functions.

    Sensation
    Contains numerous sensory receptors that detect touch, heat, cold and pain.
    Heat Regulation
    Can sweat and dilate blood vessels near the surface to release heat.
    Absorption
    Provides protection from a number of environmental hazards including absorption of ultraviolet light (UV) before it damages deeper tissues
    Protection
    Protects the body from mechanical trauma, provides a barrier to pathogens and can secrete antimicrobial substances
    Excretion/secretion
    Makes a small, but significant contribution to the excretion of metabolic waste
    Synthesis
    Melanin, keratin, VitaminD
    Skin cutane/o, dermato/o, derm/o
    Intact skin is the first line of defense for the immune system. It also waterproofs the body and provides the major receptors for sensation.
    Sebaceous Glands seb/o
    Secrete sebum (oil) to lubricate the skin and prevent bacterial growth.
    Sweat Glands hidr/o
    Secrete sweat to regulate body temperature and water content and excrete some metabolic waste.
    Hair pil/i, pil/o
    Aids in controlling the loss of body heat.
    Nails onchy/o, ungu/o
    Protects the dorsal surface of the last bone of each finger and toe.

    Layers of the Skin

    Epidermis Outermost layer
    Contains squamous epithelial tissue for protection against abrasion
    Has keratin, a tough and water-repellent protein
    Contains melanocytes, cells that produce the pigment melanin which gives skin color and also protects cells against UV damage
    Contains no blood supply and thus continually sheds at surface
    Dermis Beneath epidermis
    Made of connective tissue
    Contains blood vessels which nourish the layers above, nerves for sensation, and, sweat glands, sebaceous glands (oil), hair roots, and lymph vessels
    Hypodermis
    Also known as the subcutaneous layers
    Mostly composed of adipose tissue (fat)
    Allows for independent movement of skin but stabilization to underlying tissues
    Rich blood supply

    Accessory Structures of the Skin

    Sweat Glands
    Apocrine glands are found at hair follicles, mostly near armpits and the groin and release an odorous liquid.
    Eccrine glands are watery glands found all over the body for thermoregulation, most numerous on the hands and feet.
    Sebaceous Glands
    Release an oil (sebum) which helps lubricate the skin and as slightly acidic to discourage bacterial growth.
    Nails
    Protective keratin plates which cover the fingers and toes.
    Hair
    Protective rod-like structures made of dead cells and hard keratin protein.
    Color is determined by melanin.
    Arrector Pili Muscle
    Tiny muscle fibers attached to the hair follicles that cause the hair to stand erect; Contract in response to cold or fright

    Medical Specialties Related to the Integumentary System

    Dermatologist
    Physician specializing in diagnosing and treating disorders of the skin.
    Plastic Surgeon
    Physician specializing in surgical restoration and reconstruction of body structures.
    Cosmetic Surgeons
    Plastic surgeons; Perform operations that are usually done for aesthetic rather than medical reasons such as breast augmentation, liposuction, and facelifts.

    Epidermoid cyst (sebaceous cyst)

    Anhidrosis (hypohidrosis)

    Trichomycosis axillaris

    Alopecia areata
    autoimmune disorder that attacks the hair follicles
    Onychocryptosis
    Ingrown toenail
    Albinism
    Genetic condition.
    Deficiency or absence of pigment.
    Chloasma
    melasma or the mask of pregnancy
    Vitiligo
    Skin condition resulting from the destruction of melanocytes
    Contusion
    Injury to tissues without breaking the skin.
    Accumulation of blood within the skin.
    Ecchymosis
    Also known as a bruise.
    Large, irregular area of purplish discoloration.
    Hematoma
    usually caused by an injury; swelling of clotted blood trapped in the tissues; often named for the area where it occurs (example: subungual hematoma)
    Macule (macula)
    discolored flat spot; less than 1 cm in diameter, such as a freckle
    Nodule
    solid, raised skin lesion
    Papule
    small, raised red lesion
    Plaque
    scaly, solid raised area
    Verrucae (warts)
    small, hard skin lesions
    A wheal (welt)
    small bump that itches; can appear as urticaria (hives)
    Abscess
    closed pocket containing pus; caused by a bacterial infection
    Purulent
    producing or containing pus
    Cyst
    abnormal sac containing gas, fluid, or a semisolid material
    Pustule (pimple)
    small, circumscribed lesion containing pus
    Vesicle
    small blister containing watery fluid; less than 0.5 cm in diameter
    Bulla
    large blister; usually more than 0.5 cm in diameter
    Abrasion
    superficial layers of skin are scraped or rubbed away
    Fissure
    groove or crack-like break in the skin
    Laceration
    torn or jagged wound or an accidental cut wound
    Ulcer
    open lesion resulting in tissue loss around the edges
    Eczema (atopic dermatitis)
    result of a malfunction of the body’s immune system
    Contact dermatitis (CD)
    localized rash; caused by repeated contact with an irritant
    Psoriasis (Ps)
    Red papules covered with silvery scales
    Urticaria (hives)
    Itchy wheals caused by an allergic reaction
    Xeroderma (xerosis)
    excessively dry skin
    Gangrene
    death of tissue
    Impetigo
    highly contagious bacterial skin infection
    Tinea (ringworm)
    fungal infection
    Scabies
    infestation of itch mites-Cause small, itchy bumps and blisters;Burrow into the top layer of human skin to lay their eggs
    Callus
    thickening of part of the skin
    Keloid
    abnormally raised or thickened scar
    Lipoma
    benign, slow-growing fatty tumor
    Malignant melanoma (MM)
    Known as melanoma; the most serious type of skin cancer; occurs in the melanocytes
    Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)
    Malignant tumor of the basal cell layer of the epidermis
    Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)
    Begin as skin lesions; Appear to be sores that will not heal; May have a crusted look
